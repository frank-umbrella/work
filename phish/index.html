Phishing Awareness

How to spot suspicious emails, fake sign-in pages, and fake 2FA prompts — and what to do if something looks off.

For Google Workspace (Gmail) users

Step 1Spot the warning signs in the email

Most phishing emails share a few patterns. If you see two or more of these, slow down before you click anything.

  • Urgency or fear. “Your account will be suspended in 24 hours.” “Unusual sign-in — verify now.”
  • Unexpected request, even from a known name. A boss or coworker asking you to buy gift cards, change banking details, or read a vague document.
  • Display name vs real address don’t match. The name says “IT Support” but the address is support@random-domain.com.
  • Look-alike domains. g00gle.com, googIe.com (capital I), google-secure.com.
  • Generic greeting. “Dear user” or “Dear customer” from a service that knows your name.
  • Unexpected attachment. Especially .zip, .html, .htm, .iso, .lnk, or a PDF that asks you to “sign in to view.”
  • QR codes in the email body. Attackers use these to push you off your work computer onto your phone, where the URL is harder to inspect.

Step 2Check the link before you click

Hover your mouse over any link (or long-press on mobile) and read the real URL that pops up. If you didn’t expect it, don’t click.

A real Google sign-in page is always on one of these domains:

  • accounts.google.com — the only page that should ask for your Google password.
  • myaccount.google.com — your account settings.

Anything else — google-login.com, google.support-verify.net, docs-googl.com — is fake. The padlock in the address bar only means traffic is encrypted; it does not mean the site is real.

Tip: if a link in an email claims to go to Google, ignore it and type mail.google.com directly into your browser instead.
Example: a fake email pretending to be from Google
Inbox
Action required: unusual sign-in attempt — verify within 24 hours
Google Account Security <noreply@g00gle-account.com>

Hi user,

We detected a sign-in to your account from an unrecognized device in Lagos, Nigeria. To prevent your account from being suspended in the next 24 hours, please verify your identity now.

Verify your account
Hover shows the link actually goes to: https://account-google.support-verify.tk/signin?id=8f3ac42
Sender domain is g00gle-account.com (two zeros) — not google.com.
Generic greeting and a scary location are designed to push you to act fast.
Tight deadline (“24 hours”) is a classic urgency lever.
The visible “Verify” button hides a link to a totally unrelated domain.

Step 3Watch out for fake 2FA pages

Modern phishing kits show you a copy of the real Google sign-in page in real time. You type your password, then your 2FA code, and the attacker passes both to Google as you do it — and walks away with your session.

To avoid this:

  • Read the URL bar every time a Google sign-in screen appears. If it’s not accounts.google.com, close the tab.
  • Use Google Prompt or a security key when you can. A passkey or security key won’t work on a fake domain — that is the protection.
  • Never approve a Google sign-in prompt you didn’t start. If your phone buzzes asking “Is it you?” and you weren’t signing in, tap No, it’s not me and change your password.
  • Never share a 2FA code over email, chat, or phone. Google support will never ask for it.
Example: a fake Google sign-in page
https://account-google.support-verify.tk/signin
Real Google sign-in is on accounts.google.com
Sign in
you@company.com
••••••••••••
The URL bar shows support-verify.tk, not accounts.google.com. Always read the bar.
The padlock is green — phishing sites have HTTPS too. It only proves the connection is encrypted, not that the site is real.

Step 4If something feels off, report it

You don’t need to be sure it’s phishing. “This feels weird” is enough — we’d rather check ten safe emails than miss one bad one.

  • In Gmail on the web, open the message, click the three-dot menu next to Reply, and pick Report phishing.
  • If you already clicked a link or entered a password, tell IT right away. The faster we know, the more we can stop.
  • Verify unusual requests through a second channel — call the person on a number you already have, don’t reply to the email.

Set up 2-Step Verification

If you haven’t already, turn on 2-Step Verification on your Google account.

  1. Sign in at myaccount.google.com.
  2. Open Security → How you sign in to Google.
  3. Click 2-Step Verification and follow the prompts.
  4. Add a second method (phone prompt, Authenticator app, or a security key).
Open 2-Step Verification Help

Prefer a passkey? Set one up at myaccount.google.com/signinoptions/passkeys — it’s the strongest protection against fake sign-in pages.

For Microsoft 365 (Outlook) users

Step 1Spot the warning signs in the email

Most phishing emails share a few patterns. If you see two or more of these, slow down before you click anything.

  • Urgency or fear. “Your mailbox is full — verify in 24 hours or lose access.” “Unusual sign-in detected.”
  • Unexpected request, even from a known name. A boss or coworker asking you to buy gift cards, change banking details, or open a vague document.
  • Display name vs real address don’t match. The name says “Microsoft” but the address is no-reply@some-mailer.io.
  • Look-alike domains. rnicrosoft.com (rn looks like m), microsoft-secure.com, 0ffice365.com.
  • Shared OneDrive / SharePoint “document” links from people who don’t normally send you files.
  • Unexpected attachment. Especially .html, .htm, .zip, .iso, .lnk, or a PDF that opens to a sign-in page.
  • QR codes in the email body. Attackers use these to move you to your phone, where the URL is harder to read.

Step 2Check the link before you click

Hover your mouse over any link (or long-press on mobile) and read the real URL that pops up. If you didn’t expect it, don’t click.

A real Microsoft 365 sign-in page is always on one of these domains:

  • login.microsoftonline.com — work and school accounts.
  • login.live.com — personal Microsoft accounts.
  • account.microsoft.com / mysignins.microsoft.com — account settings.

Anything else — microsoft-verify.com, office365-login.net, secure-outlook.com — is fake. The padlock in the address bar only means traffic is encrypted; it does not mean the site is real.

Tip: if a link in an email claims to go to Microsoft 365, ignore it and type outlook.office.com or portal.office.com directly into your browser instead.
Example: a fake email pretending to be from Microsoft 365
Inbox
[Action Required] 12 messages held in quarantine — release before deletion
Microsoft 365 Security <admin@office365-portal-mail.com>

Dear user,

You have 12 messages held in your Microsoft 365 quarantine. They will be permanently deleted in 24 hours unless you review and release them now.

Review your messages

Sender domain is office365-portal-mail.com — nothing real from Microsoft comes from a random “-mail.com” domain.
Generic greeting and a fabricated message count to make you click before checking.
The countdown (“deleted in 24 hours”) is the urgency lever.
The “Review” button leads to a totally unrelated overseas domain.

Step 3Watch out for fake 2FA pages

Modern phishing kits show you a copy of the real Microsoft sign-in page in real time. You type your password, approve the Authenticator prompt, and the attacker passes both to Microsoft as you do it — and walks away with your session.

To avoid this:

  • Read the URL bar every time a Microsoft sign-in screen appears. If it’s not login.microsoftonline.com, close the tab.
  • Number-matching prompts. When Microsoft Authenticator asks you to type a number, that number should be on the screen you are signing in from. If you didn’t just open a sign-in page, do not type any number — tap No, it’s not me.
  • Don’t fall for push fatigue. If your phone keeps pinging with sign-in prompts you didn’t start, deny every one and report it. Don’t approve to make the noise stop.
  • Never share a code over email, chat, or phone. Microsoft support will never ask for it.

Set up MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)

Turn on MFA from the Microsoft Security Info page using your work account.

  1. Sign in at mysignins.microsoft.com/security-info.
  2. Click Add sign-in method.
  3. Pick Microsoft Authenticator (recommended) or another option.
  4. Follow the prompts to scan the QR code in the Authenticator app.
Open Security Info aka.ms/mfasetup

Get the Microsoft Authenticator app first: microsoft.com/…/authenticator-app

Proofpoint Essentials

If your account is protected by Proofpoint Essentials, use this to log in and release legitimate emails from quarantine.

Sign in

Open Proofpoint Essentials
  1. Sign in with your full work email address.
  2. Use the password Proofpoint sent you, or click Forgot password? to set one.

Release a legitimate email from quarantine

Option A — from the Quarantine Digest email (sent on a schedule):

  1. Open the digest from Proofpoint Essentials.
  2. Find the message you were expecting.
  3. Click Release to deliver it once, or Release & Allow Sender to deliver it and let future messages from that sender through.

Option B — from the portal:

  1. Sign in (link above).
  2. Go to Logs → Quarantine (or Quarantined Messages).
  3. Tick the messages you want to release.
  4. Click Release, or Release & Allow Sender if you trust them going forward.
Only release messages you were actually expecting. If you’re not sure, leave it — quarantined messages are deleted automatically after a set period and won’t hurt anything sitting there.
